# human-insulin-BLAST-Analysis
Bioinformatics project involving BLAST analysis of Human Insulin protein sequence using NCBI BLAST to identify homologous sequences, sequence similarity, and evolutionary relationships.

## Project Overview

This project presents a bioinformatics analysis of the Human Insulin protein sequence using NCBI BLASTP (Basic Local Alignment Search Tool for Proteins). The study aims to identify homologous protein sequences, evaluate sequence similarity, and understand the evolutionary conservation of insulin across different organisms.

## Objectives

- Retrieve the Human Insulin protein sequence from UniProt.
- Perform protein sequence alignment using NCBI BLASTP.
- Identify homologous insulin proteins in other species.
- Analyze sequence identity, query coverage, alignment scores, and E-values.
- Explore the evolutionary relationships of insulin proteins.

- UniProt Knowledgebase (UniProtKB)

## Methodology

### Step 1: Sequence Retrieval
The Human Insulin protein sequence was obtained from the UniProt database (UniProt ID: P01308).

### Step 2: BLASTP Analysis
The amino acid sequence was submitted to NCBI BLASTP and searched against the non-redundant protein database (nr).

### Step 3: Result Analysis
The following BLAST result sections were analyzed:

- Description
- Graphic Summary
- Alignments
- Organism Distribution
- Taxonomy

### Step 4: Documentation
Relevant screenshots and observations were collected and organized for reporting.

## Results and Observations

- Human insulin showed highly significant matches with insulin proteins from several species.
- Top hits included primates such as gorilla, chimpanzee, and orangutan.
- Query coverage reached 100% for the best matches.
- Extremely low E-values indicated statistically significant alignments.
- The protein was identified as part of the insulin/IGF-like protein family.

## Biological Significance

The high sequence conservation observed among mammals highlights the critical biological role of insulin in glucose metabolism and energy regulation. Conserved sequences often indicate important functional regions that have been maintained through evolution.
